Typography

The civic case for legible typography on public information surfaces

Jane Doe
May 21, 2026 9 min read Typography

Most state-built information surfaces — DMV portals, tax forms, court records, transit signage — still rely on system defaults set in the early 2000s. The defaults serve poorly. They serve poorly when the operator is reading on a 60% scaled mobile viewport at 6 a.m., they serve poorly when the operator is reading with corrected vision, and they serve poorly when the operator is reading under stress about an outcome that affects whether they get to keep their housing1.

What's missing is not aesthetic refinement. What's missing is a contract: that the agency commissioning the surface treats typography as a measurable variable, not as a defaults problem to be inherited.

Typography on civic surfaces is not a taste question. It is a question of whether the operator can read the document in the time they have.

Three contracts

I want to propose three contracts2 that public-information operators can adopt without procurement overhead.

The first contract is measure. Type at 16 pixels on a 600-pixel column hits the readable measure target3. Most public surfaces blow that out by 200% or more, because the underlying CSS is grid-based instead of measure-based.

The second contract is contrast. WCAG floors are floors, not targets — and yet most agencies treat them as both. A 4.5:1 ratio against a near-white surface reads acceptable at 18 pixels and unreadable at 14 pixels.

The third contract is fallback. Civic surfaces should not depend on web fonts loading. Every primary-prose typeface should have a system-stack fallback that the page is willing to ship with permanently.

  1. The literature on reading-under-stress is established; see Larson & Picard's 2014 work on typography and cognitive load. ↩
  2. Each is small enough to fit inside an existing accessibility-statement document. None of the three require new procurement. ↩
  3. 45–75 characters per line is the canonical readable measure (Bringhurst). On the web this is roughly 28–46rem at 16px body. ↩
